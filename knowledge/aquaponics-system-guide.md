# Aquaponics System Guide

## The Nitrogen Cycle — System Foundation

The entire aquaponics system depends on beneficial bacteria converting toxic ammonia into plant-available nitrate:

```
Fish eat food
    ↓
Fish excrete ammonia (NH3/NH4+) — TOXIC to fish
    ↓
Nitrosomonas bacteria convert NH4+ → NO2- (nitrite) — ALSO TOXIC
    ↓
Nitrobacter/Nitrospira convert NO2- → NO3- (nitrate) — PLANT FERTILIZER
    ↓
Plants absorb nitrate → clean the water → fish thrive
```

**This cycle is why aquaponics works.** The bacteria live primarily in the biofilter media and on any wetted surface. They are the most vulnerable component of the system — protect them.

### Bacterial Health Requirements
- pH: 6.5 – 8.5 (crash below 6.5 or above 9.0)
- Temperature: 20 – 30°C optimal; slow but functional 15 – 35°C
- Dissolved oxygen: > 2 mg/L (aerobic process)
- Ammonia as food source: too little slows growth; too much indicates imbalance
- NO antibiotics, chlorine, or heavy metal contamination

### System Cycling (New Systems)
Cycling = establishing the bacterial colony before stocking fish at full density.

**Signs of a cycled system:**
- Ammonia stays near 0 ppm (bacteria consuming it)
- Nitrite stays near 0 ppm (bacteria consuming it)
- Nitrate steadily accumulates
- This process takes **4 – 8 weeks**

**Cycling methods:**
1. **Fishless cycling:** Add pure ammonia source (ammonium chloride) daily; dose to keep NH3 at 2-4 ppm until it drops to near 0 within 24 hours
2. **Fish cycling:** Start with very low fish density (25% of final); feed sparingly; monitor daily
3. **Seeded cycling:** Add established biofilter media or commercial nitrifying bacteria; significantly faster

## System Types

### Deep Water Culture (DWC) / Raft System
- Plants float on foam rafts over deep (30-40 cm) water channels
- Fish tank separate from plant channels; water flows from fish tank → settle tank → plant channels → fish tank
- **Pros:** High plant density, excellent for leafy greens, efficient water use, stable water quality
- **Cons:** Requires separate fish and plant components; biofilter often a separate unit

**Best for:** Lettuce, herbs, leafy greens, bok choy, kale

### Media Bed (Flood and Drain)
- Plants grow in gravel, expanded clay, or similar media
- Water floods and drains periodically (timed or bell siphon)
- Media acts as biofilter + plant support + solids filter simultaneously
- **Pros:** Simple all-in-one system; good for diverse crops; handles solids well
- **Cons:** Media can waterlog; not as scalable; solids accumulate in media over time

**Best for:** Tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, herbs, strawberries; small to medium scale

### NFT (Nutrient Film Technique)
- Thin film of water flows through sloped gutters; plant roots hang in the flow
- **Pros:** Water and space efficient; good for leafy greens
- **Cons:** Less stable water quality; poor solids handling; roots can overheat; requires consistent flow
- Less common in aquaponics due to solids management challenges

### Hybrid Systems
- Combination of media bed + DWC or NFT + DWC
- Media bed handles solids + acts as biofilter
- DWC or NFT for high-density plant production
- **Best of both worlds:** Most resilient and productive for mixed crop production

## System Balance and Stocking

### Fish-to-Plant Ratio (by feeding rate)
The most reliable system balance metric:

**Feeding rate density (FRD):** grams of fish food per day per square meter of plant area

| System Type | FRD Target | Notes |
|------------|-----------|-------|
| DWC (leafy greens) | 60 – 100 g/m²/day | Higher plant density possible |
| Media bed | 40 – 80 g/m²/day | Media provides more biofilter capacity |
| NFT | 15 – 20 g/m²/day | Limited by small surface area |

**Rule of thumb:** If nitrate is consistently below 20 ppm with established plants → increase feeding rate or fish density. If consistently above 100 ppm → add more plant area or reduce feeding.

### Fish Stocking Density
- **Light stocking:** 20 – 40 kg/m³ — robust, high water quality, low risk
- **Medium stocking:** 40 – 60 kg/m³ — balanced, typical for established systems
- **High stocking:** 60 – 100 kg/m³ — high productivity, demands excellent aeration and monitoring

**Never exceed biofilter capacity.** Signs of overloading: rising ammonia despite good aeration.

### Common Fish Species

**Tilapia** — most common, highly resilient
- Temperature: 22 – 30°C (cold-sensitive below 15°C, can die)
- Tolerates wide pH range and moderate ammonia
- Omnivore — accepts many feed types
- Fast growth rate

**Trout** — cold water, premium product
- Temperature: 12 – 18°C (heat-sensitive above 20°C)
- High oxygen demand (> 7 mg/L)
- More pH sensitive than tilapia
- Great for colder climates

**Catfish** — very hardy
- Temperature: 20 – 30°C
- Tolerates low oxygen better than most
- Bottom feeder — good for consuming uneaten food

**Koi/Goldfish** — ornamental or hobby
- Wide temperature tolerance
- Moderate nutrient production
- Good for small systems; not food fish

**Barramundi/Bass** — premium food fish
- High temperature (26 – 30°C)
- Fast growth rate
- More demanding water quality requirements

## Common System Problems

### Problem: Ammonia Rising Steadily
**Causes:**
1. Overfeeding — reduce to what fish consume in 5 minutes, twice daily
2. Overstocked relative to biofilter capacity
3. Dead fish decomposing — remove immediately
4. Bacterial die-off from chlorine in water change, antibiotic residue, pH crash, or temperature shock

**Response (in order):**
1. Stop feeding immediately
2. Remove any dead fish or rotting material
3. Do partial water change (20-30%) with dechlorinated water
4. Aerate aggressively
5. Check pH — keep above 6.5 for bacteria
6. Add commercial beneficial bacteria product

### Problem: Nitrite Elevated with Normal Ammonia
**Cause:** Nitrosomonas bacteria (ammonia → nitrite) are working but Nitrobacter (nitrite → nitrate) are lagging. This is common in new systems during mid-cycle.

**Response:**
- Add non-iodized salt (1 g/L) as fish protection
- Be patient — Nitrobacter establishment takes 2-3 weeks after Nitrosomonas
- Do not overfeed during this period
- Add seeded biofilter media from established system if available

### Problem: Nitrate Very Low (< 10 ppm) with Many Plants
**Cause:** Underloaded system — fish not producing enough nutrient for plant demand

**Response:**
- Increase fish stocking density
- Increase feeding rate (ensure fish are eating all food within 5 minutes)
- Reduce plant density temporarily
- Consider fish waste extract from denser system

### Problem: Nitrate Very High (> 150 ppm) with Healthy Fish
**Cause:** Overloaded system — too many fish or too much feed for plant area

**Response:**
- Increase plant area
- Reduce feeding rate
- Partial water change to dilute
- Harvest mature plants and replant

### Problem: pH Crashing (Rapidly Falling)
**Most likely:** Low alkalinity (KH) with active nitrification

**Response:**
1. Test KH immediately — if < 50 ppm, this is the cause
2. Add potassium bicarbonate (KHCO3) — calculate dose needed
3. Temporarily reduce feeding to slow nitrification rate
4. Continue monitoring daily

### Problem: pH Rising Consistently
**Most likely:** Algae bloom (CO2 consumption), carbonate dissolving from media, low fish load

**Response:**
- Check for algae — green water or algae mats
- Cover system to block light if algae is cause
- Consider reducing carbonate media if using
- Increase fish feeding rate slightly

## Nutrient Supplementation Schedule

Most aquaponics systems need regular supplementation of these nutrients:

| Supplement | Frequency | When | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|------|-------|
| Chelated Iron (Fe-DTPA) | Weekly | When Fe < 2 mg/L | Most commonly needed |
| Potassium Bicarbonate | As needed | When K < 10 mg/L or pH too low | Dual benefit: K + alkalinity |
| Calcium Chloride | As needed | When Ca < 40 mg/L | Check after water changes |
| Epsom Salt (MgSO4) | As needed | When Mg < 10 mg/L or interveinal chlorosis seen | |
| Potassium Sulfate | For fruiting crops | During fruiting stage | Extra K demand |

**Tip:** Test before supplementing. Over-supplementing creates antagonism between nutrients.

## Water Change Guidance

**When to do water changes:**
- Nitrate consistently > 150 ppm (dilution)
- After disease treatment (remove medication)
- Salinity buildup from heavy salt use
- Replenishing evaporated water (top up, not full change)

**Water change rules:**
- Always dechlorinate — chlorine kills nitrifying bacteria
- Match temperature to system water (± 2°C)
- Never change more than 30% at once unless emergency
- Test after change — municipal water chemistry varies seasonally

**Note:** Most aquaponics systems are designed to need minimal water changes. If you're changing water frequently, investigate the root cause.
