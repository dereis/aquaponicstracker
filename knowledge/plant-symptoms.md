# Plant Symptom Identification Guide

## How to Use This Guide

1. Identify which leaves are affected: **older/lower leaves** or **newer/upper leaves**
2. Identify the symptom type: yellowing, browning, spots, distortion, wilting
3. Note the pattern: uniform, interveinal (between veins), marginal (edges), tip only
4. Cross-reference with recent water parameter readings

**Key diagnostic principle:**
- Symptoms on **OLD LEAVES** → mobile nutrient deficiency (N, P, K, Mg, Zn)
- Symptoms on **NEW LEAVES/growing tips** → immobile nutrient deficiency (Ca, B, Fe, Mn, Cu, S)

---

## Symptom Index

### 1. Yellowing Leaves (Chlorosis)

#### Old/Lower Leaves Turn Yellow Uniformly
**Most likely: Nitrogen (N) deficiency**
- Uniform pale yellow starting from leaf tip, spreading to whole leaf
- Progresses from oldest leaves upward as deficiency worsens
- Plant appears overall pale or light green before individual leaf yellowing
- Stems may be thin and weak
- **Aquaponics context:** Nitrate < 20 ppm; underloaded system; too many plants for fish load
- **Check:** Nitrate levels; if < 20 ppm, system is underloaded
- **Action:** Increase fish feeding or stocking; check nitrogen cycle is complete

#### Old/Lower Leaves Yellow Between Veins (Veins Stay Green)
**Most likely: Magnesium (Mg) deficiency**
- Interveinal chlorosis — clear contrast between yellow tissue and green veins
- Older leaves affected first (Mg is mobile)
- May progress to necrotic (dead, brown) patches in severe cases
- Sometimes reddish-purple tinge
- **Check:** Mg levels; optimal 10-30 mg/L
- **Action:** Add Epsom salt (MgSO4) — 25-50 mg/L; also check K and Ca aren't excessive (antagonism)

#### Old/Lower Leaves Yellow at Edges/Tips
**Most likely: Potassium (K) deficiency**
- Brown or yellow leaf tips and margins
- Scorching/burning appearance at leaf edges
- Yellowing progresses from edge inward
- Older leaves affected first
- **Aquaponics context:** K almost always needs supplementation; fish waste provides almost none
- **Check:** K levels; < 10 mg/L indicates deficiency
- **Action:** Add potassium bicarbonate (KHCO3) or potassium sulfate; target 20-40 mg/L

#### New/Young Leaves Are Yellow While Old Leaves Stay Green
**Most likely: Iron (Fe) deficiency**
- Interveinal chlorosis on NEW young leaves (opposite of Mg — this is the key distinguisher)
- Veins stay green/yellow-green while tissue between veins turns pale yellow
- In severe cases, entire young leaf turns yellow or white
- No necrosis typically (unlike Mn)
- **Aquaponics context:** Most common micronutrient deficiency; high pH (>7.5) causes Fe lockout
- **Check:** Iron levels (< 2 mg/L); more importantly, check pH (> 7.5 means Fe unavailable)
- **Action:** Add chelated iron (Fe-DTPA for pH < 7.5; Fe-EDDHA for pH > 7.5); lower pH if possible

#### New/Young Leaves Are Yellow With Green Veins + Small Brown Spots
**Most likely: Manganese (Mn) deficiency**
- Interveinal chlorosis similar to Fe but with small necrotic (dead) spots
- Young leaves primarily affected
- Spots may enlarge and merge in severe cases
- Hard to distinguish from Fe without testing; try Fe supplement first
- **Check:** pH (Mn unavailable above pH 7.5 like Fe); test Mn levels
- **Action:** Add manganese sulfate (MnSO4) 0.5-1 mg/L; check pH

#### All Leaves Uniformly Light Green/Yellow-Green
**Most likely: Nitrogen deficiency (early stage) or Sulfur deficiency**
- Sulfur: uniform yellowing of NEW growth (immobile)
- Nitrogen: uniform pallor of OLD growth first
- Distinguish by which leaves are most affected
- **Sulfur:** Rare in aquaponics; check if using chelated iron or Mg supplements (some contain S)
- **Action for N:** See nitrogen deficiency above

### 2. Brown, Scorched, or Necrotic Tissue

#### Brown Leaf Tips on Older Leaves → Potassium Deficiency or Salinity
- Marginal tip scorch on outer leaves
- Often accompanied by yellowing of leaf edges
- Check K levels and also check for salt accumulation

#### Brown Leaf Edges (Margin Scorch) on Older Leaves → K Deficiency
- Distinguished from K deficiency with tip scorch: edges brown, progressing inward
- Older leaves worst affected

#### Brown Spots on Leaves → Multiple Possible Causes
- **Fungal disease:** Spots with defined edges, often with yellow halo; may spread
- **Bacterial:** Waterlogged, irregular spots; may have yellow halo
- **Manganese toxicity:** Brown spots especially on older leaves; check Mn levels and pH
- **Calcium deficiency:** Can cause tip burn (especially in lettuce, inner leaves)
- **Check:** Look for pattern — random (disease) vs. consistent/patterned (nutrient)

#### Tip Burn in Lettuce (Brown Inner Leaf Edges)
**Most likely: Calcium deficiency or low transpiration**
- Brown, papery edges on inner/young leaves
- NOT a disease — a physiological calcium disorder
- Caused by insufficient Ca reaching growing points via transpiration
- **Aquaponics-specific causes:**
  - Low Ca levels (< 40 mg/L)
  - Low air circulation reducing transpiration
  - Heat stress
  - Rapid growth outpacing Ca delivery
- **Action:** Increase Ca supplementation; improve air circulation; ensure Ca > 60 mg/L

#### Blossom End Rot (Dark Sunken Area at Fruit Bottom) — Tomatoes, Peppers
**Calcium deficiency** — even if Ca levels seem adequate
- Ca delivery to fruit depends on transpiration; highly demanding crop areas
- **Causes:**
  - Low Ca in water (< 40 mg/L)
  - Irregular watering causing disrupted Ca flow
  - Overly fast growth
  - Root damage limiting Ca uptake
- **Action:** Add calcium chloride (CaCl2) to raise to 60-80 mg/L; ensure consistent water flow; foliar spray 0.5% CaCl2 on affected plants

### 3. Distorted, Malformed, or Stunted Growth

#### Distorted/Twisted/Curled Young Leaves
**Most likely: Calcium or Boron deficiency**
- Young tissue is being formed incorrectly — cell wall integrity issues
- Ca deficiency: new leaves distorted, cupped, irregular margins
- Boron deficiency: new leaves very small, thick, brittle; growing tips die; hollow stems
- **Distinguishing:** Boron causes more severe tip dieback; Ca causes more distortion
- **Check:** Ca > 40 mg/L; for B deficiency, look for multiple growing tip deaths

#### Growing Tips Die Back (Apex Necrosis)
**Most likely: Calcium or Boron deficiency**
- Growing point (apical meristem) dies
- New lateral growth emerges ("witches broom" appearance with B)
- **Check:** Ca levels and KH (Ca); B rarely tested but worth considering if Ca correction fails
- **Action:** Calcium chloride supplementation; foliar spray Ca; B as boric acid (0.1-0.2 mg/L — B window is narrow)

#### Small, Narrow Leaves, Short Internodes, Stunted
**Most likely: Zinc (Zn) deficiency or general stunting**
- Zn: small leaves, short internodes, mottled chlorosis on middle-aged leaves
- **Caution:** Zn is toxic to fish at relatively low concentrations — use foliar spray only
- Also consider: root damage from low DO, temperature extremes, or pathogens

#### Overall Stunted Growth Despite Normal Color
**Consider multiple causes:**
- pH outside optimal range (even slightly) reducing nutrient uptake
- Low temperature (< 18°C slows plant metabolism)
- Root zone issues: low DO, algae blocking light to roots
- Insufficient nitrate (< 20 ppm)
- Allelopathy in mixed species systems

### 4. Color Changes Beyond Yellowing

#### Purple/Red Coloration on Leaves or Stems
**Most likely: Phosphorus (P) deficiency or cold stress**
- Purple pigment (anthocyanin) accumulates when P metabolism is disrupted
- Undersides of older leaves first, then older leaf surfaces
- Dark green upper surface, red/purple underside
- Also occurs in cold temperatures (< 15°C) even with adequate P
- **Aquaponics context:** P deficiency is uncommon; check temperature first
- **Action:** Warm the system; P supplementation rarely needed in aquaponics

#### Overall Dark Green, Lush Growth, Slow Flowering
**Possible: Nitrogen excess**
- Too much nitrate drives vegetative growth at expense of fruiting
- Soft, succulent tissue susceptible to pests
- **Action:** Reduce fish feeding or density; increase plant density to consume N

#### Blue-Green Tint on Young Leaves
**Possible: Copper (Cu) deficiency**
- Rare in aquaponics
- Young leaves with unusual blue-green color, wilting at tips
- **Important:** Never use copper compounds in aquaponics water — highly toxic to fish

---

### 5. Root Symptoms

#### Brown/Slimy Roots
**Most likely: Root rot (Pythium) from low dissolved oxygen**
- Healthy roots: white, firm, extensive
- Diseased roots: brown, soft, slimy, reduced
- **Cause:** Dissolved oxygen below 3-4 mg/L in root zone
- **Action:** Increase aeration; check water pump and flow rates; consider hydrogen peroxide treatment (0.5 ml/L of 3% H2O2) as emergency measure

#### Pink/Reddish Roots
**Normal:** Pink tinge from exposure to light (anthocyanin response)
- Ensure root zone is shaded from light to prevent algae growth

#### Limited Root Development, Short Roots
**Consider:** Phosphorus deficiency; low temperature; pH extremes; waterlogging without O2

## Quick Diagnostic Decision Tree

```
WHICH LEAVES ARE AFFECTED?
│
├─ OLD/LOWER leaves → Mobile nutrient problem
│   ├─ Uniform yellow → NITROGEN deficiency (check nitrate levels)
│   ├─ Yellow between veins, veins stay green → MAGNESIUM deficiency  
│   ├─ Brown tips/edges → POTASSIUM deficiency
│   └─ Purple underside → PHOSPHORUS deficiency (or cold stress)
│
└─ NEW/YOUNG leaves or growing tips → Immobile nutrient problem
    ├─ Yellow between veins, no spots → IRON deficiency (check pH + Fe)
    ├─ Yellow between veins + brown spots → MANGANESE deficiency
    ├─ Distorted, cupped, irregular → CALCIUM deficiency
    ├─ Dead tip, small thick leaves → BORON deficiency
    └─ Uniform yellow (new growth only) → SULFUR deficiency

PLUS CHECK pH FIRST: If pH > 7.5 → Fe, Mn, Zn lockout regardless of levels
```

## Species-Specific Notes

### Lettuce and Leafy Greens
- Tip burn (inner leaf brown edges) = Ca/transpiration issue
- Bolting (premature flowering) = temperature, day length, or nitrate excess
- Target nitrate: 40-80 ppm

### Tomatoes and Peppers
- Blossom end rot = Ca delivery failure
- Very high K demand during fruiting — supplement aggressively
- Mg deficiency common during heavy fruiting
- Target nitrate: 50-100 ppm; K: 30-60 mg/L

### Basil
- Purple pigmentation on leaves = P deficiency or cold; ensure temperature > 20°C
- Yellowing = usually Fe or N
- Very sensitive to cold water — keep temperature > 22°C

### Strawberries
- Tip burn = Ca deficiency
- Poor fruit set = B deficiency or pollination issues
- Runners compete with fruiting — manage plant density
